# Competitive and GEO/AEO Patterns

Durable structural patterns for how a small site competes for search ranking and AI-answer citation against larger, better-resourced competitors. Distilled from a live competitive research pass, generalized to remove anything specific to the original site or industry.

**Re-derive specifics live.** The patterns below are structural and reasonably durable. The competitor names, exact queries, and citability mechanics in the source pass are not, treat them as illustrative, not as a list to reuse. Re-run the actual competitive research (Step 3 of the main skill) for whatever site is being audited.

## Taxonomy depth beats one broad page

Sites that win competitive queries in a niche rarely do it with a single general landing page. The winning pattern is usually a taxonomy: a hub page for the broad category, then individual leaf pages for each sub-topic, role, product line, or location, cross-linked with breadcrumbs. Each leaf page tends to be long-form and specific: a definition of the sub-topic, how it differs from adjacent ones, and concrete supporting detail (data, examples, pricing, whatever's relevant to the niche).

A single-page or shallow site structurally can't compete for more than one or two queries at a time, no matter how well-written that one page is. Each new, genuinely distinct page is a new indexable and citable unit.

## Hyper-local or hyper-specific ownership

A small player can credibly own a narrow angle that a bigger generalist competitor only mentions in passing, most commonly a specific location, a specific sub-role, or a specific niche combination. Check whether competitors reference the narrow angle only briefly (one paragraph in a blog post) versus owning a dedicated page for it. A gap there, especially one the site has a genuine authenticity claim to (actually based there, actually specializes in that sub-role), is often the fastest realistic win for a small site against bigger competitors.

## Data-backed pages earn links and citations

Original, falsifiable data (a stated sample size, a disclosed methodology, a specific number) draws both backlinks and AI-answer citations in a way generic advice content doesn't. If the site has access to real data nobody else has published (comp ranges, survey results, usage stats), a page built around it and its methodology tends to outperform equivalent effort spent on generic informational content.

## Founder/author entity as a ranking and citation signal

Converting a founder or key person's bio into a real "author entity" (a linked author page, `Person` schema, consistent bylines, a linked LinkedIn or equivalent professional profile via `sameAs`) is a durable signal search engines and AI systems use for credibility (loosely, experience/expertise/trust signals). This is cheap to do if the structured data already exists and is usually underused.

## The listicle/roundup gap (GEO-specific)

"Top N [category]" roundup articles are exactly the kind of content AI answer engines pull from when asked to name options in a category, they're structured, comparative, and easy to extract from. Check whether the site's specific niche has any such roundups, and if so whether the site (or a comparable player) appears in them. A niche with active roundups but no listicle presence for this site is often a faster GEO win via outreach/PR to get included than pure organic content work, since it borrows an already-citable page's authority instead of building citability from zero.

## What makes a source citable to an AI answer engine

Distinct from classic ranking factors: clear entity definition (structured data that lets a system resolve who/what the business is unambiguously), specific named and ideally sourced data points (not vague claims), Q&A-formatted content that maps naturally to how a user might phrase a question, and third-party mentions or press (not just owned-site copy asserting its own authority). `llms.txt` or similar proposed standards may or may not have real adoption by any given AI vendor at any given time, check current status before recommending implementing one, it has a history of being proposed without broad production support.

## Consumer/candidate-side queries are usually owned by aggregators

Broad "[thing] near me" or "[role] jobs"-shaped queries are typically dominated by large marketplaces or aggregators (job boards, review sites, big-box comparison sites) that a small site cannot outrank head-on with equivalent content. Don't spend the site's limited content budget chasing these; redirect that budget toward the high-intent commercial and informational buckets where a specific, credible small player can actually win a spot.

## Crowded vs. winnable, judged by direct inspection

Never classify a query as "crowded" or "winnable" from assumption. Open the actual top results and look at real depth and freshness. A query can look crowded by volume of competitors bidding on it in ads while being genuinely thin in organic content, or vice versa. The classification only holds for as long as the SERP does, re-check it on each audit rather than trusting a prior pass's labels.
